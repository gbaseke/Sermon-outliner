# Is It God's Plan?

**Text**: Jeremiah 29:1-11 (KJV)
**Theme**: God always has a plan for His people, but there will always be voices seeking to seduce them away from it — and the faithful must learn to wait, work, and trust while the plan unfolds.
**Duration**: 60-90 minutes

---

## Introduction

**Hook — The Letter Nobody Wanted to Receive**

Imagine you are sitting in a foreign land. You did not choose to be there. The soldiers of Nebuchadnezzar marched you out of your home, away from the temple, away from everything familiar. And then a letter arrives — not from the king of Babylon, but from the old prophet Jeremiah back in Jerusalem. You unroll it expecting a word of deliverance, a promise of quick rescue, a "thus saith the LORD, I am coming to get you out." Instead, the letter says: *build houses and dwell in them. Plant gardens. Get married. Have grandchildren. Seek the peace of the city where I have sent you.*

If that does not sound like the word you were praying for, you are not alone. The captives in Babylon had false prophets whispering in their ears that the exile would be short — two years at most. Jeremiah's letter was not what they wanted. But it was what they needed.

And that, beloved, is our subject this morning: **Is it God's plan?**

Because somebody is always telling you what God is about to do. Somebody always has a word, a vision, a prophecy, a shortcut out of your season. And the question you must be equipped to answer is not simply "Is God able?" — of course He is able. The question is: "Is *this* the plan of God, or is someone seducing me away from it?"

**Scripture Reading — Jeremiah 29:1-11 (KJV)**

> "Now these are the words of the letter that Jeremiah the prophet sent from Jerusalem unto the residue of the elders which were carried away captives, and to the priests, and to the prophets, and to all the people whom Nebuchadnezzar had carried away captive from Jerusalem to Babylon; (After that Jeconiah the king, and the queen mother, and the eunuchs, the princes of Judah and Jerusalem, and the carpenters, and the smiths, were departed from Jerusalem;) By the hand of Elasah the son of Shaphan, and Gemariah the son of Hilkiah, (whom Zedekiah king of Judah sent unto Babylon to Nebuchadnezzar king of Babylon) saying, Thus saith the LORD of hosts, the God of Israel, unto all that are carried away captives, whom I have caused to be carried away from Jerusalem unto Babylon; Build ye houses, and dwell in them; and plant gardens, and eat the fruit of them; Take ye wives, and beget sons and daughters; and take wives for your sons, and give your daughters to husbands, that they may bear sons and daughters; that ye may be multiplied there, and not diminished. And seek the peace of the city whither I have caused you to be carried away captives, and pray unto the LORD for it: for in the peace thereof shall ye have peace. For thus saith the LORD of hosts, the God of Israel; Let not your prophets and your diviners, that be in the midst of you, deceive you, neither hearken to your dreams which ye cause to be dreamed. For they prophesy falsely unto you in my name: I have not sent them, saith the LORD. For thus saith the LORD, That after seventy years be accomplished at Babylon I will visit you, and perform my good word toward you, in causing you to return to this place. For I know the thoughts that I think toward you, saith the LORD, thoughts of peace, and not of evil, to give you an expected end."

**Road Map**

This morning we will walk through four great pillars of truth from this passage:

1. How did God's people end up in Babylon, and what does that season type for us?
2. What is God's instruction during the waiting season?
3. Who are the voices trying to pull you away from the plan?
4. What is the nature of the plan, and how sure is "an expected end"?

## I. The Road to Babylon — Understanding the Captive Season

### A. Why and When Were They Carried Captive?

The Babylonian captivity did not happen overnight. It came in waves — first under Jehoiakim (Daniel 1:1-2), then under Jeconiah (also called Jehoiachin), and finally the full destruction of Jerusalem under Zedekiah in 586 BC. This letter in Jeremiah 29 is addressed to those carried away with Jeconiah — the craftsmen, the smiths, the elders, the priests. These were not the refuse of society; these were skilled, gifted, significant people.

> "The LORD sent him against them to destroy it, according to the word of the LORD, which he spake by his servants the prophets." — 2 Kings 24:2

Here is a fact that will either trouble you or settle you: **God caused it.**

Verse 7 of our text says "whom I have *caused* to be carried away." Not permitted — *caused.* They did not stumble into Babylon; God directed their steps there. Disobedience opened the door (see Deuteronomy 28:36), but God is sovereign over every door. He does not lose control of His story.

**Type and Shadow — Jerusalem and Babylon**

Jerusalem in this passage types the place of blessing, covenant relationship, and the presence of God. Babylon types the world-system — a place of distraction, foreign gods, and pressure to conform. Every believer knows something of a Babylonian season: a season where the familiar comforts of spiritual flourishing have been stripped away, and you find yourself in a strange land singing the LORD's songs (Psalm 137:4).

But do not miss this: Babylon was not the end of the story. It was the *corridor* to the next chapter.

### B. Zedekiah — The King Who Would Not Listen

> "And king Zedekiah the son of Josiah reigned instead of Coniah the son of Jehoiakim, whom Nebuchadrezzar king of Babylon made king in the land of Judah." — Jeremiah 37:1

Zedekiah is a fascinating and tragic figure. He is the king still sitting in Jerusalem while the captivity is in progress. He *wants* to hear from God — he privately consults Jeremiah (Jeremiah 37:17) — but he lacks the courage to obey what God says. He is a man caught between the fear of God and the fear of man.

Notice that in verse 3 of our text, Zedekiah sends the very envoys who carry Jeremiah's letter to Babylon. He is, in a sense, delivering a message of submission he himself refuses to live by. What a picture of the person who will forward the sermon but will not apply it.

The lesson from Zedekiah: it is not enough to *know* the plan of God. You must have the courage to *stay* in it.

**Application**: Are you a Zedekiah? Have you heard the word of the LORD clearly, acknowledged it privately, but refused to fully surrender to it because of what others might say? Today is the day for the private acknowledgment to become public obedience.

## II. What to Do While You Wait — The Instructions for the Captive Season

### A. Build, Plant, and Multiply

> "Build ye houses, and dwell in them; and plant gardens, and eat the fruit of them; Take ye wives, and beget sons and daughters..." — Jeremiah 29:5-6

The natural reading of these words strikes the reader as almost shockingly ordinary. No dramatic instructions. No strategy for escaping Babylon. No plan for political uprising. Just: *be fruitful where you are.*

This is one of the most counter-intuitive principles in all of Scripture. The flesh says: "I refuse to invest in this season. This is not where I am supposed to be. I am not going to build a house because I will not be here long enough to need one." And so the flesh folds its arms, sits down in the corner, and waits in bitterness.

But God says *build.* God says *plant.* God says *multiply.*

**Nature Illustration — The Seed in Winter Ground**

When a gardener plants a bulb in autumn, the ground is cold and the conditions look barren. Nothing about the frozen soil suggests that spring is coming. But the wise gardener does not withhold the seed just because the season looks wrong. The bulb goes into the ground precisely *because* spring is coming — and it takes time to prepare. The captive season is the winter ground. What you plant now will bloom when the appointed time arrives.

### B. Seek the Peace of the City

> "And seek the peace of the city whither I have caused you to be carried away captives, and pray unto the LORD for it: for in the peace thereof shall ye have peace." — Jeremiah 29:7

This is a remarkable verse. God tells His exiled people to pray *for Babylon*. Not to pray *against* it. Not to pray for its destruction so they can leave sooner. Pray for its peace — because their welfare is tied to it.

This is the New Testament principle of 1 Timothy 2:1-2 written centuries early:

> "I exhort therefore, that, first of all, supplications, prayers, intercessions, and giving of thanks, be made for all men; For kings, and for all that are in authority; that we may lead a quiet and peaceable life in all godliness and honesty." — 1 Timothy 2:1-2

Notice the logic: God's people are not called to be agitators in the culture; they are called to be *intercessors* for it. You are not just passing through this world — you are salt and light in it. Your presence should make the city better.

**Application**: Stop cursing your circumstances and start praying for them. Your "Babylon" — your difficult workplace, your difficult neighborhood, your difficult season — is not a prison you must escape. It is a mission field you have been assigned. Seek its peace. Your peace is wrapped up in it.

## III. The Seducers — Recognizing Voices That Lead You Away from the Plan

### A. The False Prophets in Their Midst

> "For thus saith the LORD of hosts, the God of Israel; Let not your prophets and your diviners, that be in the midst of you, deceive you, neither hearken to your dreams which ye cause to be dreamed. For they prophesy falsely unto you in my name: I have not sent them, saith the LORD." — Jeremiah 29:8-9

God does not merely give His people a plan; He warns them that others will show up claiming to have a better one. The false prophets in Babylon were telling the exiles what their itching ears wanted to hear: this captivity will be short, deliverance is just around the corner, do not bother settling in. They spoke *in the name of the LORD* — this is critical. They did not say "thus saith Baal." They said "thus saith the LORD." The danger was not paganism dressed as paganism; the danger was a counterfeit word dressed as a true one.

**1 John 4:1 — The Command to Test**

> "Beloved, believe not every spirit, but try the spirits whether they are of God: because many false prophets are gone out into the world." — 1 John 4:1

Notice: John does not say *suspect* every spirit. He says *try* every spirit. The word "try" here means to test and prove, the way a metallurgist tests metal for purity. You do not throw away every piece of metal — you test it. You do not reject every prophetic voice — you test it.

How do you test it? By the Word of God (Isaiah 8:20), by the fruit it bears (Matthew 7:20), and by whether it exalts Christ (1 John 4:2-3).

**Type and Shadow — 1 Kings 13: A Prophet Lying to a Prophet**

In 1 Kings 13 there is one of the most sobering stories in all of Scripture. A man of God from Judah comes to Bethel with a clear, specific command from God: deliver the message, eat no bread, drink no water, and return by a different way. He delivers the message faithfully. Then an old prophet in Bethel comes to him with a counterfeit word: "I am a prophet also, and an angel told me to bring you back to eat bread with me."

The man of God disobeys the word of God based on the word of another prophet — and it costs him his life. The old prophet was not an enemy soldier. He was not a pagan priest. He was a *prophet.* He used the language of heaven to deliver a lie from the pit.

The lesson is devastating in its clarity: **the source of a message does not automatically validate the message.** A prophet can lie to a prophet. A minister can mislead a minister. A spiritual-sounding word can be thoroughly unspiritual. You must know the word of God well enough to recognize when any voice — regardless of its credentials — contradicts it.

### B. The Three Unclean Spirits — A New Testament Echo

> "And I saw three unclean spirits like frogs come out of the mouth of the dragon, and out of the mouth of the beast, and out of the mouth of the false prophet." — Revelation 16:13

In the end, deception is demonic in its origin. These spirits go out to the kings of the earth to gather them for battle — but the mechanism is the *mouth.* Words. Prophecies. Declarations. Voices. The enemy has always understood that if he can control what you hear, he can control where you go.

The captives in Babylon were not being attacked with swords in this passage. They were being attacked with words. "You will not be here seventy years. God would not do that to you. Leave now. Do not build. Do not plant. Do not pray for this city." Every one of those whispers was a frog from the pit dressed in prophetic clothing.

**Type and Shadow — The Wolf in Sheep's Clothing**

> "Beware of false prophets, which come to you in sheep's clothing, but inwardly they are ravening wolves." — Matthew 7:15

A wolf in sheep's clothing is not dangerous because it looks like a wolf. It is dangerous because it looks like a sheep. It moves with the flock. It knows the vocabulary. It bleats at the right moments. The danger is not obvious — it is *subtle.* And the only way to distinguish it is not by the outward appearance but by the fruit (Matthew 7:16).

### C. The Great Falling Away — A Warning for the Last Days

> "Let no man deceive you by any means: for that day shall not come, except there come a falling away first..." — 2 Thessalonians 2:3

Paul warns Timothy that in the last days, perilous times will come (2 Timothy 3:1), and the falling away — the great apostasy — will precede the day of the LORD. Notice: people do not fall away from something they never embraced. They fall away from what they once believed. The seduction is not directed at the world; it is directed at the church.

The question "Is it God's plan?" becomes most urgent when the falling away is occurring around you. When the people you sat next to in the pew are departing the faith, when the voices calling you to compromise are multiplying, when the promise of a quick fix or an easier road sounds almost reasonable — that is when you must anchor yourself to the word of God.

**How Do You Know Someone Is Sent by God?**

A true messenger sent by God will:
1. Not contradict the written Word of God (Isaiah 8:20 — "To the law and to the testimony: if they speak not according to this word, it is because there is no light in them.")
2. Exalt the Lord Jesus Christ (1 John 4:2 — "Every spirit that confesseth that Jesus Christ is come in the flesh is of God")
3. Bear fruit consistent with their message over time (Matthew 7:20 — "Wherefore by their fruits ye shall know them")
4. Lead you *into* obedience, not away from it (Deuteronomy 13:1-4 — even a sign-and-wonder worker who leads you after other gods is to be rejected)

**Application**: Get into the Word deeply enough that a counterfeit word feels wrong in your spirit before you can even articulate why. That is the discernment God promises to those who abide in Him (1 John 2:27). You cannot counterfeit what you have never known — but you also cannot recognize a counterfeit of what you have never studied.

## IV. The Certainty of the Plan — "An Expected End"

### A. The Thoughts of God Toward You

> "For I know the thoughts that I think toward you, saith the LORD, thoughts of peace, and not of evil, to give you an expected end." — Jeremiah 29:11

This is perhaps the most beloved verse in the entire book of Jeremiah — and it is written to *captives.* This is not a prosperity verse for a comfortable life. This is a covenant promise for people in chains. The glory of it is that God makes this declaration not when everything is going well, but when everything appears to have gone wrong.

Three observations about the thoughts of God:

**First — They are God's thoughts, not ours.** "I *know* the thoughts that *I think* toward you." The thoughts are not hidden from God. He has not forgotten you. He has not lost the blueprint. While the captives were confused about their situation, God was not confused. He knew exactly what He was doing and why.

**Second — They are thoughts of peace, not evil.** The word "peace" here is the Hebrew *shalom* — wholeness, completeness, well-being, nothing missing and nothing broken. God's default intention toward His people is not punishment but wholeness. The captivity itself was not God's hatred — it was God's discipline, and discipline is reserved for sons (Hebrews 12:6-7).

**Third — They are pointed toward "an expected end."** The word translated "expected end" is more literally a *hope and a future* — a destination that is already decided. The end of the story is not in question. The plan has a conclusion written into it before the first page was turned.

### B. The Seventy Years — A Fixed Appointment

> "For thus saith the LORD, That after seventy years be accomplished at Babylon I will visit you, and perform my good word toward you, in causing you to return to this place." — Jeremiah 29:10

The number seventy is precise. Not "eventually." Not "when things improve." *Seventy years.* God set a specific appointment on the calendar of heaven, and the captivity could not last one day longer than that appointment allowed. Daniel understood this — he read Jeremiah's prophecy, calculated the years, and set his face in prayer (Daniel 9:2-3) because he knew the appointed time was near.

There is something deeply stabilizing about knowing that your difficult season has a fixed end on God's calendar. The enemy wants you to believe you will be in Babylon forever. God says there is a number attached to your season — and when that number is accomplished, He will *visit* you, He will *perform* His word, and He will cause you to *return.*

**Nature Illustration — The Monarch Butterfly**

The Monarch butterfly undertakes one of the most astonishing migrations in all of creation. It travels up to three thousand miles from its summer grounds to its wintering place in Mexico. But here is the extraordinary detail: no single butterfly makes the round trip. The butterfly that arrives in Mexico is not the one that left. It takes multiple generations. Yet they arrive at the *same trees* their great-grandparents left from — because the destination is encoded in them before they are born.

The people of God carry the destination of their expected end encoded in the new nature given by God. You may not live to see the full flowering of what God promised — but the promise does not fail. The expected end is sure.

### C. The Return from Captivity — Fulfillment as Proof

The captives were indeed returned. Cyrus the Persian king issued the decree in 538 BC (Ezra 1:1-3), precisely fulfilling Isaiah's prophecy (Isaiah 44:28) — which was spoken *before Cyrus was born.* God called this pagan king by name a century before he took the throne, because God's plans do not depend on the cooperation of earthly kings to be accomplished.

> "Thus saith the LORD to his anointed, to Cyrus, whose right hand I have holden, to subdue nations before him..." — Isaiah 45:1

The fulfillment of the Babylonian captivity and return is not merely ancient history. It is a stake driven into the ground of your faith: **God has done it before. He will do it again.** The same God who kept the clock running for seventy years in Babylon is keeping the clock running in your situation.

**Application**: Stop trying to shorten the season. Stop listening to voices that promise a quick exit from the plan of God. The seventy years were seventy years — not sixty-eight, not sixty-nine. God's timing is exact. Your job is not to accelerate the clock; your job is to build, plant, multiply, and pray until the appointed time arrives.

## Conclusion

**Landing the Plane — Trust in the Captive Season**

We began this morning with a question: *What do you do while you wait for the plan of God to be accomplished?*

Jeremiah's letter gives us the answer, and it is not what the flesh wants to hear. You *build.* You *plant.* You *multiply.* You *seek the peace* of the very place you did not choose to be in. You *pray.* And you *test every voice* that comes to you with a shortcut out of your season, because there is always a false prophet in Babylon who will tell you what you want to hear.

The plan of God is not in jeopardy. It does not need your panic to succeed. It does not need the false prophets to be right. It does not need Zedekiah to be brave. It runs on the schedule of the God who called Cyrus by name before he was born, who counted every year of the captivity, and who sent a letter to people in chains saying: "I know the thoughts I think toward you."

Those are not the thoughts of a God who has forgotten you. Those are not the thoughts of a God who lost control of the story. Those are the thoughts of a God who sees the end from the beginning — who already knows how the chapter closes — and is telling you today: *the expected end is sure.*

**Closing Challenge**

If you are in a Babylonian season this morning — a season of displacement, waiting, confusion, or pressure — receive this letter from Jeremiah as a letter from the LORD to you.

Stop waiting to live until you get out. *Build where you are.* Stop resenting the city you are in. *Pray for it.* Stop listening to every voice that promises you a quick deliverance. *Try the spirits.* And rest in this: the God who has thoughts of peace toward you has already written your expected end — and it is good.

**Invitation**

If you have never placed your trust in the God who has a plan — the God who sent His own Son as the ultimate fulfillment of every promise, the one in whom all the plans of God are "yea and amen" (2 Corinthians 1:20) — today is the day. You do not have to figure out the plan on your own. You simply have to come to the One who holds it.

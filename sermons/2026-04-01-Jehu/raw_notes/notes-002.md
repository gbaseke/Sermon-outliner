Themes for "Jehu"

  1. Jezebel's Judgment (dominant theme, ~6 results)

  Jehu appears almost exclusively in the context of Jezebel's execution. The narrative of her painting her face  
  to meet Jehu, and his command to throw her from the window, is retold repeatedly — always as the fulfillment of   Elijah/Elisha's prophecy and as God's righteous judgment on wickedness.

  2. Jehu as the Uncompromising Man of God

  Jehu is held up as a model of spiritual toughness — a man who could not be seduced or manipulated. The phrase  
  "If we had more men like Jehu today, we'd have less Jezebels" (1959-07-08) captures this clearly. He is        
  contrasted with compromised, soft leadership.

  3. Warning Against Female Vanity / Worldliness

  The image of Jezebel painting her face to seduce Jehu becomes a repeated warning about cosmetics and worldly   
  fashion in women (1960-06-10, 1959-07-08, 1956-10-05). Jezebel is the only woman in Scripture said to have     
  painted her face — and God fed her to dogs.

  4. Modern Church as Jezebel / Denominationalism

  In the 1963 message on the Seals, Jehu's role becomes eschatological: Jezebel typifies the modern church       
  (Catholic and Protestant alike, united in denominationalism), and Jehu's judgment foreshadows coming divine    
  judgment on that system.

  5. Righteous Lineage Producing Righteous Men

  One passing reference (1956-10-05) notes Jehu was "the son of Jehoshaphat — the righteous man with the right   
  daddy behind him." This touches a recurring theme in Branham's preaching: godly parentage shapes godly         
  character.

  6. Frantic Pace of Modern Life

  A single outlier reference (1957-06-29) uses the phrase "driving through the streets like Jehu" — drawn from 2 
  Kings 9:20 ("he driveth furiously") — as a metaphor for the neurotic, directionless rush of modern life.       

  ---
  Summary: Jehu is overwhelmingly used as a symbol of uncompromising, prophetically-sanctioned judgment — chiefly   against Jezebel as a type of seduction, worldliness, and corrupt religion. The secondary application is       
  consistently pastoral: calling for bold, unmanipulable leadership in the church.

  Jehu — A KJV Biblical Portrait

  ---
  Who He Was

  Full name: Jehu ben Jehoshaphat ben Nimshi. Commonly called "son of Nimshi" — suggesting his grandfather carried more name  
  recognition than his own father, hinting at an obscure family background.

  Name meaning: "Yahweh is He" — a name that described his divine mission far better than it described his personal piety.    

  Career before the crown: A professional soldier his entire adult life. He served as charioteer and aide-de-camp to King     
  Ahab, rode in close personal proximity to the king, and later commanded the garrison at Ramoth-Gilead under Ahab's son      
  Joram. He knew the Omride dynasty from the inside — its corruption, Naboth's murder, Jezebel's persecution of the prophets. 
  He was no outsider. He was a witness.

  The calling — predestined long before deployment: Jehu's appointment did not begin with Elisha. It began with Elijah on     
  Mount Horeb years earlier:

  "Jehu the son of Nimshi shalt thou anoint to be king over Israel." — 1 Kings 19:16 KJV

  God built his instrument long before he deployed it. Jehu had a decades-long preparation he knew nothing about.

  The anointing — 2 Kings 9:1-10: Elisha sent a young prophet to Ramoth-Gilead to anoint Jehu privately, pour oil on his head,   and commission him with a specific fourfold mandate:
  1. Smite the house of Ahab
  2. Avenge the blood of the prophets Jezebel killed
  3. Kill Jezebel — "the dogs shall eat Jezebel in the portion of Jezreel"
  4. Cut off every male of Ahab's house

  Notably, Elisha told the young prophet to anoint Jehu — and then flee immediately. Even the prophetic community was wary of 
  this man's volatile temperament.

  Unique distinction: Jehu is the only northern king ever formally anointed. Every other northern king either seized power by 
  coup or inherited it. The anointing gave his rebellion divine authorization and set him apart as more than a usurper.       

  ---
  What He Did

  The Furious Ride — 2 Kings 9:14-20

  His first act after anointing was to seal off Ramoth-Gilead so no messenger could warn Joram. He then drove toward Jezreel  
  at a pace so reckless the watchman identified him from a distance:

  "The driving is like the driving of Jehu the son of Nimshi; for he driveth furiously." — 2 Kings 9:20 KJV

  The Hebrew word for "furiously" (shigga'on) is the same word used earlier to describe the young prophet as a "mad fellow."  
  This was not admiration. It was recognition of a well-known, defining character trait.

  The Killing of Joram — 2 Kings 9:21-26

  When Joram rode out asking "Is it peace, Jehu?", the answer came theologically:
  "What peace, so long as the whoredoms of thy mother Jezebel and her witchcrafts are so many?"

  Jehu drew his bow and shot Joram through the heart — then ordered the body cast into Naboth's field, explicitly quoting     
  Elijah's prophecy that he had personally witnessed years before. He remembered the word. He connected the dots.

  The Death of Jezebel — 2 Kings 9:30-37

  Jezebel painted her face and looked out a window — a last act of defiance or seduction. Jehu called to her eunuchs: "Who is 
  on my side? who?" They threw her down. Jehu rode over her body. He went in to eat and drink. When servants were sent to bury   her, only skull, feet, and hands remained — the dogs had eaten her exactly as Elijah prophesied. Jehu's response was to    
  cite the prophecy. He knew the Scriptures. He just didn't always obey them.

  The Seventy Sons of Ahab — 2 Kings 10:1-11

  Jehu intimidated the rulers of Samaria into delivering all seventy of Ahab's sons. Seventy heads arrived in baskets.        
  Standing at the gate before the display, Jehu made a theological argument to the people: "The LORD is righteous." He then   
  killed everyone in Jezreel connected to the house of Ahab.

  Jehonadab in the Chariot — 2 Kings 10:15-17

  En route to Samaria, Jehu met Jehonadab son of Rechab — a renowned ascetic, founder of the Rechabite movement, revered for  
  his piety. Jehu's famous question:

  "Is thine heart right, as my heart is with thy heart?... If it be, give me thine hand." — 2 Kings 10:15 KJV

  He took Jehonadab up into his chariot and said: "Come with me, and see my zeal for the LORD." This moment is a window into  
  Jehu's soul (see Hidden Needs below).

  Destruction of Baal Worship — 2 Kings 10:18-28

  His most strategically brilliant act. He called a national assembly of all Baal worshippers, pretending he intended to serve   Baal more than Ahab ever had. When the house of Baal was full and the doors sealed, his men killed every worshipper inside.   He demolished the temple and made it a latrine. Organized Baal worship was eradicated from Israel. God commended him for   
  this.

  ---
  His Successes

  1. Executed God's judgment on the house of Ahab — fulfilling a prophecy decades in the making, precisely as spoken
  2. Killed Jezebel — ending the most powerful engine of Baal promotion and prophetic persecution in Israel's history
  3. Destroyed Baal worship institutionally — demolished the temple, killed the priests, ended organized Baalism
  4. Received divine commendation — "thou hast done well in executing that which is right in mine eyes... thy children of the 
  fourth generation shall sit on the throne of Israel" (2 Kings 10:30)
  5. Longest-reigning northern king — 28 years; established the longest-lasting dynasty in the northern kingdom (four
  generations)
  6. Confirmed by archaeology — The Black Obelisk of Shalmaneser III (c. 827 BC, British Museum) depicts Jehu prostrating     
  before the Assyrian king — the earliest known depiction of an Israelite in ancient art

  ---
  His Mistakes and Failures

  1. Continued the Sins of Jeroboam

  The text's most damning verdict:
  "But Jehu took no heed to walk in the law of the LORD God of Israel with all his heart: for he departed not from the sins of   Jeroboam, which made Israel to sin." 
  — 2 Kings 10:31 KJV

  Jeroboam's golden calves at Bethel and Dan were politically convenient — they kept the northern people from going to        
  Jerusalem and potentially switching loyalty to the Davidic throne. 
  Jehu destroyed Baal worship (foreign, associated with his   enemies) but preserved the golden calf system (homegrown, politically stabilizing). 
  He eliminated the idolatry inconvenient   to him and kept the idolatry useful to him. 
  This exposes the calculated nature of his "reform."

  2. Paid Tribute to Assyria

  The Black Obelisk records Jehu prostrating himself before Shalmaneser III — in 841 BC, the same year his reign began. 
  Having   dismantled Israel's Phoenician alliance through Jezebel's dynasty, he turned to Assyria for cover. 
  He purchased security   with tribute, depleting national resources and dignity.

  3. Territory Loss

  "In those days the LORD began to cut Israel short: and Hazael smote them in all the coasts of Israel." — 2 Kings 10:32 KJV  

  Loss of Transjordanian territory — Gilead, Bashan, Gad, Reuben — was direct divine consequence for incomplete obedience.    

  4. The Shearing House Killings

  The killing of forty-two relatives of Ahaziah of Judah (2 Kings 10:12-14) had no explicit divine mandate. Commentators      
  describe it as a "cruel excess" — Jehu operating beyond his commission, sweeping away anyone who might constitute a future  
  threat.

  5. Deception as Method

  He lied to the Baal worshippers, claiming to be their champion, to trap them. Some commentators defend this as military     
  stratagem against idolaters. Others flag it as a character marker — ends-justify-the-means pragmatism that reveals a man for   whom truth is a tool, not a value.

  6. The Judgment in Hosea

  "I will avenge the blood of Jezreel upon the house of Jehu." — Hosea 1:4 KJV

  This creates a theological tension with 2 Kings 10:30 (where God commends him). The resolution scholars offer: What Jehu did   was right as an act; what corrupted it was selfish ambition and mixed motives. He watched God judge Ahab's apostate dynasty   — and then led his own dynasty into the same apostasy. He was punished not for the killings, but for failing to learn what 
  the killings were meant to teach.

  His Hidden Needs (What Drove Him Beneath the Surface)

  Validation and Public Glory

  The most revealing text: "Come with me, and see my zeal for the LORD" (2 Kings 10:16). A man of pure motive says "Come, see 
  what the LORD is doing." Jehu says "Come, see my zeal." He needed an audience. He needed witnesses. He took Jehonadab — the 
  most respected ascetic in Israel — into his chariot not for accountability but for credibility. He wanted Jehonadab's       
  reputation as religious endorsement for his political program.

  Ambition Dressed as Obedience

  He obeyed God where obedience aligned with self-interest. He disobeyed where it conflicted with political convenience. This 
  is not the behavior of a man surrendered to God's agenda — it is the behavior of a man using God's agenda selectively.      

  The Need to Be the Story

  His narrative throughout 2 Kings 9-10 is saturated with self-reference and self-display. God is the authorizing force behind   his heroism, but Jehu is the protagonist. This is a subtle but devastating inversion — he wanted the story to be more about   Jehu.

  Fear Beneath the Fury

  The furious driving, the relentless momentum, the refusal to pause — these are also the behaviors of a man who cannot afford   to stop, reflect, or be questioned. 
  He moved fast because stillness was dangerous. The reckless energy was, in part, a way 
  of foreclosing second thoughts — his own and everyone else's.

  Institutional Loyalty Over Personal Devotion

  His refusal to dismantle Jeroboam's golden calves was not theological ignorance — he clearly knew the Scriptures. It was    
  institutional allegiance. He needed the northern kingdom distinct from Judah. He chose his throne over his God — not        
  dramatically, not with Ahab's brazenness, but quietly, structurally, systematically.

  No Personal Relationship with God

  The text never records Jehu praying. Not once. He quoted prophecy. He cited Scripture. He acted, killed, declared, displayed   
  — but never sought. He was instrumentally religious. God was a commission, a source of authority, a resource 
  — never the   object of his love or fellowship. He was used by the Spirit without ever being filled by it.

  ---
  Summary Theological Portrait

  Jehu represents the frightening possibility of being God's instrument without being God's servant — of carrying out divine  
  purposes while harboring an unredeemed interior.

  Four things his life confronts directly:

  1. Zeal is not obedience. He had more zeal than nearly any figure in Kings. But zeal bent toward self-display and selective 
  execution is not whole-hearted surrender.
  2. You can know the Scriptures and still not follow them. He quoted Elijah's prophecy. He knew Naboth's story. Knowledge    
  informed his actions; it never transformed his heart.
  3. Partial obedience is a form of disobedience. God did not reward the portion; He judged the whole.
  4. He asked Jehonadab the only question that finally matters — and never asked it of himself. "Is thine heart right?"
